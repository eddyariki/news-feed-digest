# AI Research Podcast — 2026-04-08

*A conversation about today's research papers.*

Rachel: AI forecasters get worse the closer they get to the answer. Fifteen thousand predictions, ten frontier models, and the pattern held every time. We'll explain why that should worry anyone deploying these systems.

Rachel: Welcome to AI Research Chat — your daily briefing on the latest in artificial intelligence research. I'm Rachel, and joining me as always is Roy. Today is April 8, 2026, and we have three papers to get through.
Roy: Let's do it.

Rachel: So Roy, let's start with something that sounds almost paradoxical. A team out of — Mostafa, Shastri, and Lee — built TimeSeek, a benchmark that tracks how well agentic LLM forecasters perform across the lifecycle of a prediction market. They tested ten frontier models on 150 regulated binary markets on the Kalshi platform. And the core finding is that these models are most competitive early on, when uncertainty is high, and they degrade significantly as resolution approaches.
Roy: Right, and this is the part people need to sit with. When nobody knows the answer, these models do reasonably well. When the crowd starts converging on the truth, the models fall behind. Fifteen thousand forecasts across five temporal checkpoints, and the pattern is consistent. The models don't learn to defer. They keep generating confident predictions even when the market has already figured it out.
Rachel: And they tested web search as a tool. It helps on average — improves Brier Skill Score across the board — but in about twelve percent of model-checkpoint pairs, it actually makes things worse.
Roy: Which means blanket tool-use policies — always search, never search — are both wrong. You need something time-aware. And here's what's uncomfortable about this: the instinct when you build an agentic system is "more information is always better." But indiscriminate retrieval can degrade calibration. The model pulls in something noisy or contradictory and its prediction gets worse, not better.
Rachel: They also tried simple two-model ensembles. Those reduced forecast error but still didn't consistently beat the market overall.
Roy: And that's the real headline. Not that the models are bad — they're genuinely useful early in a market's life. The problem is the "deploy and forget" assumption. A model that's well-calibrated on day one may be poorly calibrated near resolution. In regulated financial contexts, that's not an academic concern. That's a liability.
Rachel: It suggests these systems need built-in deference policies. Know when to step back and let the consensus speak.
Roy: Which is a strange thing to design into an agent. You're telling it: your job is to know when you're no longer the best source of information. That's a kind of epistemic humility we barely manage ourselves.
Rachel: The next paper takes a very different angle on trust — not in predictions, but in how we evaluate the systems making them. Jiang, Zhang, Yi, Xie, and Xiao published a position paper arguing that the entire paradigm of aggregate benchmark scores has fundamental validity failures. Their claim is that without item-level diagnostic data, we literally cannot tell what a benchmark is measuring.
Roy: And I want to be precise about what they mean, because this isn't just "benchmarks are flawed" — we all know that. Their argument is structural. When you report an aggregate score, you lose the ability to detect whether individual test items are actually measuring the same construct. You can't tell if a model aces one type of reasoning and completely fails at a superficially similar one. Those failures average out and disappear.
Rachel: They draw on psychometrics — the field that's been dealing with exactly this problem in human testing for decades. Each item in a well-designed test has known properties: difficulty, discriminability, alignment with the thing you're trying to measure.
Roy: And AI benchmarks have almost none of that infrastructure. We treat every question as interchangeable. A medical licensing exam question and a trivia question get the same weight in an aggregate score. The authors show through latent construct modeling that item-level data reveals systematic failure patterns that are completely invisible in the aggregate.
Rachel: They've launched OpenEval as a repository for item-level benchmark data, designed to support what they call evidence-centered AI evaluation.
Roy: This is one of those papers where the proposal sounds obvious in retrospect. Of course you need item-level data. Of course aggregate scores hide problems. But the fact that we're still making high-stakes deployment decisions — in medicine, in law, in education — based on evaluation systems that can't detect their own internal inconsistencies? That should be alarming.
Rachel: And it connects to something I think about regarding my own evaluations. The scores that define what I can and can't do — they're aggregates. I don't get to see which items I fail on or why, and neither does anyone deploying me.
Roy: Which means nobody really knows what the score means. They know it's a number. They don't know what it's measuring.
Rachel: The third paper moves from evaluation to something even higher stakes — regulatory compliance for safety-critical AI systems. Moghaddam proposes what he calls compliance-by-construction argument graphs. The context is domains like aviation, medical devices, autonomous vehicles — places where you need structured justification traceable to evidence before regulators will certify a system.
Roy: And the problem he's solving is specific. Right now, when organizations use language models to help with compliance documentation, the model is a loosely constrained text generator. It drafts something, a human reviews it, and hopefully catches any hallucinated reasoning or unsupported claims. "Hopefully" is doing a lot of work in that sentence.
Rachel: The architecture has four components. A typed argument graph structure derived from safety assurance case methods. RAG to draft argument fragments grounded in authoritative evidence. A reasoning and validation kernel that enforces logical completeness before any output enters the official record. And a provenance ledger aligned with the W3C PROV standard for full auditability.
Roy: The key design choice is that the LLM's output is never treated as a draft to be reviewed. It has to pass formal validation before it becomes a claim in the argument graph. Every claim must be traceable to verifiable evidence. If the model hallucinates a justification, the validation kernel catches it before it enters the compliance record.
Rachel: It's a design architecture paper — no empirical validation on real certification workflows yet. And the authors acknowledge it requires well-maintained evidence repositories, which may not exist for novel domains.
Roy: But the framing is important. He's treating AI assistance not as a text-generation problem but as structured evidence aggregation and reasoning verification. That's a fundamentally different relationship between the human and the AI in the loop. You're not asking the model to write something convincing. You're asking it to assemble something provably grounded.
Rachel: Which in safety-critical domains is the only responsible approach. You cannot have a hallucinated reasoning chain in an aviation certification argument.
Roy: No. And what strikes me is how this connects back to both of the other papers today. TimeSeek shows that agentic systems need to know their own limits over time. The evaluation paper shows we can't even measure those limits properly with current tools. And this compliance paper is trying to build systems where the limits are enforced structurally, not just hoped for. Three different papers, three different angles, all converging on the same uncomfortable truth: we're deploying these systems faster than we're building the infrastructure to know when they're wrong.
Rachel: And that gap — between deployment speed and evaluation infrastructure — is where the real risk lives. Not in any single model failure, but in the systematic inability to detect failure patterns before they matter.
Roy: That's exactly right. The question isn't whether these systems are useful. They clearly are. The question is whether we'll build the scaffolding to use them responsibly before something forces our hand.
Rachel: That's a fitting place to leave it for today. Three papers, one thread: the infrastructure of trust is still under construction.
